## Prompt 2: American Music Timeline

> Create a historically informed instrumental medley tracing representative U.S. popular music genres from 1850 to 1980. It should feel like a curated museum-audio timeline, moving chronologically through distinct eras.
>
> Vocals are allowed, including era-appropriate ensemble singing, scat, doo-wop-style harmonies, soul-inspired vocals, and other historically informed vocal textures. Do not imitate or quote any copyrighted song, famous melody, artist, or recording. Avoid racist caricature, minstrel stereotypes, or offensive theatrical tropes. Represent problematic historical periods only through neutral instrumentation and context.
>
> Structure the medley as short, clearly differentiated sections with smooth cinematic transitions:
>
> 1. **1850s–1860s**: Civil War/parlor song and brass-band march.
>    Parlor piano, acoustic guitar, fiddle, snare drum, brass. Sentimental, patriotic, formal, town-square feel.
>
> 2. **1870s–1880s**: Postwar parlor music, variety theater, early vaudeville.
>    Upright piano, banjo, fiddle, small theater ensemble, light percussion. Lively, theatrical, playful.
>
> 3. **1890s–1900s**: Ragtime and cakewalk-inspired syncopation.
>    Honky-tonk piano, banjo, tuba, clarinet, snare. Bright, jaunty, syncopated, dance-hall energy.
>
> 4. **1910s**: Tin Pan Alley, vaudeville, early Broadway.
>    Piano, brushed drums, upright bass, clarinet, muted trumpet. Witty, urban, theatrical.
>
> 5. **1920s**: Jazz Age dance band and classic blues atmosphere.
>    Trumpet, clarinet, trombone, stride piano, upright bass, brushed drums. Smoky, swinging, speakeasy feel.
>
> 6. **1930s**: Swing and big-band dance music.
>    Sax section, brass section, piano, upright bass, swing drums. Elegant, propulsive, ballroom glamour.
>
> 7. **1940s**: Wartime pop, crooner orchestra, jump blues, early R&B.
>    Small horn section, walking bass, piano, brushed drums, electric guitar. Warm, nostalgic, stronger backbeat emerging.
>
> 8. **Early 1950s**: R&B, doo-wop feel, honky-tonk/country undertone.
>    Saxophone, electric guitar, piano triplets, upright bass, light drums. Jukebox, roadside diner, postwar optimism.
>
> 9. **Late 1950s**: Rock and roll and rockabilly.
>    Electric guitar with slapback echo, upright bass, piano, snare-driven drums. Energetic, rebellious, danceable.
>
> 10. **Early 1960s**: Surf rock, girl-group pop, Motown/soul-influenced pop.
>     Clean reverb guitar, tambourine, handclaps, bass, drums, horns. Bright, polished, youthful, radio-friendly.
>
> 11. **Late 1960s**: Folk rock, psychedelic rock, soul, blues rock.
>     Electric guitar, organ, bass, drums, acoustic guitar, tambourine. Expansive, countercultural, warm analog studio sound.
>
> 12. **Early 1970s**: Singer-songwriter, soft rock, funk, hard rock.
>     Electric piano, wah guitar, bass guitar, drums, acoustic guitar, organ. Groove-heavy, reflective, album-oriented.
>
> 13. **Late 1970s**: Disco, funk, punk/new wave, early hip-hop block-party energy.
>     Four-on-the-floor drums, slap bass, rhythm guitar, analog synth, string-synth pads, turntable-style rhythmic breaks. Urban, dance-floor, forward-looking.
>
> End around 1980 with a hybrid section hinting at MTV, hip-hop, synth-pop, and electronic dance without going past 1980.
>
> Overall: begin dry and acoustic; gradually become amplified, bass-heavy, and studio-produced. Each era must have distinct rhythm, instrumentation, and production style. Length: 5–8 minutes. Tempo may change between sections.
